\chapter{Research Methodology}
\label{chap:analysis}

This chapter presents the research methodology applied in this thesis. It describes
the overall research design, the operationalization of the Integration Maturity Model,
the design of the survey instrument and expert interview, data collection procedure,
and the statistical methods used to analyze the collected data.

\section{Research Design and Approach}
\label{sec:design}

This thesis adopts a quantitative research design, complemented by a qualitative
expert interview. The principal research methodology is a cross-sectional survey
study, in which data is gathered from various organizations at a single point in
time. This design is appropriate for the research objective, which is to evaluate
the present distribution of IS integration maturity levels within PMO environments
in Latvian enterprises and to examine its correlations with reporting automation,
data quality, and governance standardization \cite{saunders2019}.

The research follows a deductive approach, in which the four hypotheses stated
in the Introduction are derived from the theoretical framework presented in
Chapter~1 and tested against empirical data collected from Latvian enterprises.

\section{Survey Instrument Design}
\label{sec:survey}

The survey instrument was designed to measure the core constructs of the study:
integration maturity level, reporting automation, data quality, and governance
standardization. The instrument consists of four sections.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Part A --- Organizational Profile:} Questions about the
    organization's size, industry, and PMO structure (categorical items).
    \item \textbf{Part B --- Integration Maturity Assessment:} A single
    categorical item in which respondents select the IMM level that best
    describes their PMO's IS integration approach, supplemented by
    Likert-scale items measuring specific integration practices.
    \item \textbf{Part C --- Reporting Automation and Data Quality:}
    Five-point Likert-scale items measuring the degree of reporting
    automation and perceived data accuracy and timeliness.
    \item \textbf{Part D --- Governance Standardization:} Five-point
    Likert-scale items measuring the degree of governance standardization
    in PMO processes.
\end{itemize}

All Likert-scale items use a five-point response scale ranging from
1 (Strongly Disagree) to 5 (Strongly Agree). The survey was designed in
English and distributed electronically via Google Forms. The estimated
completion time was 10--15 minutes.

\section{Expert Interview Design}
\label{sec:interviews-design}

To supplement the quantitative survey findings with qualitative depth,
one structured expert interview was conducted with an experienced project
management professional from a Latvian enterprise. The interview followed
a structured protocol with open-ended questions covering the following themes:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The current state of IS integration in the respondent's PMO environment
    \item Challenges experienced due to low integration maturity
    \item The role of BI and dashboard systems in governance standardization
    \item Perceived barriers to advancing integration maturity
    \item Practical recommendations for improving PMO IS integration
\end{itemize}

Interview responses were analyzed qualitatively using thematic analysis to
identify patterns and insights that complement the quantitative findings.
In accordance with the ethical principles outlined in Section~\ref{sec:ethics},
the interview participant is referred to anonymously in the thesis.

\section{Data Collection Procedure}
\label{sec:datacollection}

The survey was distributed to representatives of medium and large Latvian
enterprises (50+ employees) with formal PMO structures. The target sample
size was 30--50 responses, which is sufficient for the planned descriptive
and correlational statistical analyses \cite{field2018}. Respondents were
identified through professional networks, LinkedIn, and direct organizational
contacts. Only one response per organization was accepted to ensure independence
of observations.

Data collection took place over a period of four to six weeks. All responses
are anonymous and stored securely in accordance with data protection principles.
Participation was voluntary and respondents were informed of the purpose of the
study prior to completing the survey.

\section{Data Analysis Methods}
\label{sec:analysis}

The collected data was analyzed using the following statistical methods:

\textbf{Descriptive statistics.} Frequency distributions and percentages were
used to describe the distribution of integration maturity levels across the
sample. Mean scores and standard deviations were calculated for Likert-scale
items measuring reporting automation, data quality, and governance
standardization.

\textbf{Normality testing.} The Shapiro-Wilk test was applied to determine
whether the data follows a normal distribution, which informed the choice
of correlation method \cite{field2018}. As the data did not follow a normal
distribution, Spearman rank correlation was selected as the appropriate
non-parametric method.

\textbf{Correlation analysis.} Spearman rank correlation coefficients were
calculated to examine the relationships between integration maturity level
and the three dependent variables: reporting automation, data quality, and
governance standardization. Statistical significance was assessed at the
$p < 0.05$ level.

\textbf{Qualitative analysis.} The expert interview transcript was analyzed
using thematic analysis to identify recurring themes and insights that
supplement the quantitative findings.

All quantitative analyses were conducted using SPSS statistical software.

\section{Validity and Reliability}
\label{sec:validity}

To ensure the quality of the research instrument and findings, the following
measures were taken to address validity and reliability.

\textbf{Content validity.} The survey items were designed based on established
theoretical frameworks --- specifically the Integration Maturity Model proposed
in Chapter~1, the CMMI framework \cite{paulk1993}, and the data quality
dimensions from DAMA-DMBOK \cite{dama2017}. Each survey item is explicitly
linked to a research construct and hypothesis, ensuring that the instrument
measures what it is intended to measure.

\textbf{Construct validity.} The survey constructs --- integration maturity,
reporting automation, data quality, and governance standardization --- are
operationalized based on definitions from peer-reviewed literature and
established frameworks. Multiple items per construct were included to reduce
measurement error and improve construct coverage.

\textbf{Internal consistency reliability.} Cronbach's alpha was calculated
for each multi-item construct scale to assess internal consistency. All
constructs returned values above the accepted threshold of 0.70
\cite{field2018}, confirming satisfactory internal consistency of the
survey instrument.

\textbf{External validity.} The study targets a specific population ---
representatives of medium and large Latvian enterprises with formal PMO
structures. While the findings may not be directly generalizable to all
organizations globally, they are intended to be representative of the
Latvian enterprise context within the defined scope.

\section{Research Ethics}
\label{sec:ethics}

This study adheres to the following ethical principles throughout the data
collection and analysis process:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Voluntary participation.} All survey respondents and
    the interview participant participated voluntarily and were free to
    withdraw at any time without consequence.
    \item \textbf{Informed consent.} Respondents were informed of the
    purpose of the study, how their data would be used, and who would
    have access to the results before completing the survey.
    \item \textbf{Anonymity and confidentiality.} Survey responses were
    collected anonymously. No personally identifiable information was
    collected or stored. The interview participant is anonymized in the
    thesis.
    \item \textbf{Data protection.} All collected data is stored securely
    and processed in accordance with the General Data Protection Regulation
    (GDPR) and applicable Latvian data protection legislation.
    \item \textbf{Honest reporting.} All findings are reported accurately
    and without manipulation, including results that do not support the
    research hypotheses.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations of the Research Design}
\label{sec:limitations}

While this study is designed to provide a rigorous empirical assessment of
IS integration maturity in PMO environments in Latvia, several limitations
must be acknowledged.

\textbf{Self-reported data.} The survey relies on self-reported perceptions
of PMO representatives, which may be subject to social desirability bias ---
respondents may rate their organization's integration maturity higher than
it actually is. To mitigate this, the survey includes specific behavioral
indicators rather than general self-assessment questions.

\textbf{Sample size.} The target sample of 30--50 responses is sufficient
for descriptive and correlational analysis but limits the statistical power
of more advanced models and prevents subgroup analysis by industry or
organization size. Future research with larger samples would strengthen
the generalizability of findings.

\textbf{Cross-sectional design.} The study captures a snapshot of integration
maturity at a single point in time and cannot establish causal relationships
between integration maturity and the dependent variables. Longitudinal
research would be needed to confirm causal direction.

\textbf{Geographic scope.} The study is deliberately bounded to Latvian
enterprises, which limits the generalizability of findings to other national
or regional contexts. However, this scope is appropriate given the research
aim of providing Latvia-specific empirical evidence.

\textbf{Construct measurement.} Integration maturity is measured through
a self-developed instrument rather than a previously validated scale, as no
existing validated scale specifically measures IS integration maturity in
PMO environments. While the instrument is grounded in established theoretical
frameworks, its psychometric properties require further validation in
future research.

\section{Chapter Summary}
\label{sec:chap2summary}

This chapter described the research methodology applied in this thesis.
A quantitative cross-sectional survey design complemented by one qualitative
expert interview was adopted. The four-level Integration Maturity Model was
operationalized into measurable survey indicators. The survey instrument was
designed to measure integration maturity, reporting automation, data quality,
and governance standardization across PMO environments in Latvian enterprises.
A total of 40 valid responses were collected from medium and large Latvian
enterprises with formal PMO structures. The Shapiro-Wilk normality test
indicated non-normal data distribution, leading to the use of Spearman rank
correlation analysis to test the four research hypotheses. Validity, reliability,
ethical principles, and research limitations were also addressed. The results
of the empirical study are presented in Chapter~3.
