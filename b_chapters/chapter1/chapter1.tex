\section{Core Concepts and Definitions}
\label{sec:concepts}

This section defines the foundational terminology used throughout the thesis. 
Each term is defined once and used consistently in subsequent chapters.

\subsection{Project Management Office (PMO)}
A Project Management Office (PMO) is a centralized organizational unit responsible 
for defining and maintaining project management standards, governance frameworks, 
and reporting structures across an enterprise \cite{pmi2021}. According to the 
Project Management Institute, PMOs can be classified into three types based on 
their degree of control and influence:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Supportive PMO} --- provides templates, best practices, and 
    guidance with minimal direct control over projects;
    \item \textbf{Controlling PMO} --- enforces compliance with project management 
    frameworks and requires the use of specific methodologies;
    \item \textbf{Directive PMO} --- takes direct control of projects, with project 
    managers reporting directly to the PMO.
\end{itemize}

In the context of this thesis, the term PMO refers to any formal organizational 
unit in medium or large Latvian enterprises that fulfills at least a controlling 
or directive function in project governance and reporting.

\subsection{Project Management Information System (PMIS)}
A Project Management Information System (PMIS) is a software-based system or 
combination of tools used to support the planning, execution, monitoring, and 
reporting of projects within a PMO environment \cite{laudon2015}. Common PMIS 
tools include Microsoft Project, Jira, SAP, and Power BI. A PMIS may consist 
of a single integrated platform or multiple specialized tools operating in 
parallel, which introduces the challenge of information system integration.

\subsection{Information System Integration}
Information system integration refers to the process of linking two or more 
software systems so that data flows automatically between them without manual 
intervention \cite{hohpe2004}. In PMO environments, integration typically occurs 
between project tracking tools, financial systems, and reporting platforms. The 
degree of integration can range from fully manual data consolidation to fully 
automated, real-time data synchronization via Application Programming Interfaces 
(APIs) or unified platform ecosystems.

\subsection{Integration Maturity Model}
A maturity model is a structured framework that describes the progressive 
development of a capability across a defined set of levels \cite{paulk1993}. 
In the context of information system integration, a maturity model defines 
the stages through which an organization advances from basic, manual integration 
practices toward fully automated, platform-level integration. This thesis adapts 
the concept of maturity modeling to the specific context of PMO information 
systems, proposing a four-level Integration Maturity Model as the primary 
theoretical framework.

\subsection{Business Intelligence and Dashboard Systems}
Business Intelligence (BI) refers to the technologies, processes, and practices 
used to collect, integrate, analyze, and present business data to support 
decision-making \cite{negash2004}. In PMO environments, BI systems typically 
manifest as centralized dashboards that aggregate data from multiple project 
and financial systems into a unified view. The presence or absence of centralized 
BI systems is used in this thesis as an indicator of governance standardization 
maturity.

\subsection{Data Quality}
Data quality refers to the degree to which data is accurate, complete, consistent, 
and timely for its intended purpose \cite{dama2017}. In the context of PMO 
reporting, data quality is directly influenced by the level of system integration: 
manually consolidated data is more prone to errors and delays, while automated 
integration reduces the risk of inconsistencies. This thesis measures data quality 
through perceived data accuracy and timeliness as reported by PMO representatives.

\subsection{Reporting Automation}
Reporting automation refers to the use of software tools and system integrations 
to generate project status reports, dashboards, and portfolio summaries without 
manual data entry or manipulation \cite{pmi2021}. Automated reporting reduces 
the administrative burden on PMO staff and improves the timeliness and consistency 
of management information. In this thesis, reporting automation is operationalized 
as the extent to which PMO reports are generated automatically from integrated 
systems rather than compiled manually.

\subsection{Medium and Large Enterprises in the Latvian Context}
In accordance with the European Commission enterprise size classification, 
a medium enterprise is defined as an organization employing between 50 and 249 
persons, while a large enterprise employs 250 or more persons \cite{ec2003}. 
This thesis targets medium and large Latvian enterprises as they are more likely 
to have established formal PMO structures and sufficiently complex information 
system landscapes to make integration maturity a relevant concern.

\section{Related Work: Themes, Methods, and Evidence}
\label{sec:related}

This section surveys existing research on PMO digitalization, information system 
integration, and maturity models. Prior work is organized into five thematic 
strands, which are contrasted to identify the gap that the present thesis addresses.

\subsection{Traditional PMO Research and Manual Integration Practices}
Early research on Project Management Offices focused primarily on process 
standardization, governance frameworks, and the role of PMOs in improving 
project delivery outcomes \cite{hobbs2007}. In this body of literature, 
information systems were treated as secondary tools — typically spreadsheets 
and standalone reporting software — rather than integrated platforms. Manual 
data consolidation was the dominant practice, with PMO staff collecting data 
from multiple sources and compiling reports manually. While this approach 
established the theoretical foundations of PMO governance, it did not address 
the challenges of system fragmentation or the consequences of low integration 
maturity on reporting efficiency and data quality. The primary limitation of 
this strand is its failure to account for the growing complexity of modern 
PMO information system landscapes.

\subsection{API-Based and Platform-Level Integration Research}
A second strand of research examines the technical dimensions of information 
system integration, focusing on API-based connectivity and platform-level 
ecosystems \cite{hohpe2004}. Studies in this area demonstrate that organizations 
adopting API-driven integration architectures achieve significantly higher levels 
of reporting automation and data consistency compared to those relying on manual 
or file-based data transfer \cite{laudon2015}. Platform ecosystems such as 
Microsoft 365, SAP, and Jira provide native integration capabilities that reduce 
the need for manual intervention. However, this research strand is predominantly 
technical in orientation and does not systematically examine the organizational 
or governance implications of integration maturity in PMO-specific contexts. 
Furthermore, implementation costs and the technical expertise required for 
API-based integration present significant barriers for medium-sized enterprises.

\subsection{Business Intelligence and Governance Standardization}
A third research strand investigates the relationship between Business Intelligence 
(BI) systems and organizational governance \cite{negash2004}. Studies consistently 
show that organizations with centralized BI dashboards demonstrate higher levels 
of governance standardization, as real-time data visibility enables more consistent 
decision-making and performance monitoring across projects \cite{pmi2021}. This 
strand is particularly relevant to the present thesis, as it provides theoretical 
support for Hypothesis 4 — that the use of centralized BI systems is associated 
with higher governance standardization in PMO processes. The main limitation of 
this research strand is its focus on large enterprises, with limited attention 
to medium-sized organizations or specific regional contexts.

\subsection{Maturity Model Frameworks in Information Systems Research}
The fourth strand encompasses research on maturity models applied to information 
systems and organizational capabilities \cite{paulk1993}. Established frameworks 
such as the Capability Maturity Model Integration (CMMI), the Organizational 
Project Management Maturity Model (OPM3), and the Portfolio, Programme and 
Project Management Maturity Model (P3M3) provide structured approaches to 
assessing and improving organizational capabilities across defined levels. 
More recent research has extended maturity modeling to digital transformation 
contexts, proposing Digital Maturity Models (DMMs) that assess the degree of 
digitalization across multiple organizational dimensions \cite{gartner2020}. 
However, none of these frameworks specifically address information system 
integration maturity in PMO environments as a distinct construct, leaving a 
gap that the present thesis seeks to fill.

\subsection{Digital Maturity Research in Baltic and Eastern European Enterprises}
The fifth strand encompasses emerging research on digital transformation and 
maturity in Baltic and Eastern European enterprises. Studies indicate that 
Latvian and broader Baltic enterprises generally demonstrate lower levels of 
digital maturity compared to Nordic counterparts, particularly in the areas 
of process automation and system integration \cite{eurostat2023}. While some 
research has examined e-government digitalization and SME digital adoption in 
Latvia, empirical studies specifically addressing information system integration 
maturity in PMO environments remain absent from the literature. This represents 
a significant gap, as the Latvian enterprise context — characterized by a 
predominance of medium-sized organizations and a rapidly evolving digital 
landscape — may exhibit distinct integration maturity patterns that differ 
from those documented in Western European or North American studies. The present 
thesis directly addresses this gap by providing the first empirical assessment 
of PMO IS integration maturity in Latvian enterprises.

\section{Theoretical Framework}
\label{sec:theory}

This section presents the theoretical lens that grounds the empirical investigation. 
Based on the review of existing maturity models and IS integration research in 
Section~\ref{sec:related}, this thesis proposes a four-level Integration Maturity 
Model (IMM) specifically adapted to PMO information system environments.

\subsection{Foundations of the Integration Maturity Model}
The proposed model draws on three theoretical foundations. First, the staged 
progression concept from the Capability Maturity Model Integration (CMMI) 
\cite{paulk1993}, which posits that organizations advance through discrete, 
measurable levels of capability. Second, the enterprise integration patterns 
framework \cite{hohpe2004}, which classifies integration mechanisms by their 
degree of automation and architectural sophistication. Third, the data quality 
dimensions framework \cite{dama2017}, which links integration architecture to 
data accuracy, completeness, and timeliness outcomes.

\subsection{The Four-Level Integration Maturity Model}
The Integration Maturity Model proposed in this thesis defines four levels of 
IS integration maturity in PMO environments, as presented in 
Table~\ref{tab:imm-levels}.

\begin{table}[h]
  \caption{Four-Level Integration Maturity Model for PMO Information Systems}
  \label{tab:imm-levels}
  \centering
  \begin{tabular}{p{0.08\linewidth}p{0.15\linewidth}p{0.25\linewidth}p{0.22\linewidth}p{0.20\linewidth}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Level} & \textbf{Name} & \textbf{Integration Mechanism} & 
    \textbf{Reporting} & \textbf{Governance}\\
    \midrule
    1 & Initial & No integration; fully manual data consolidation via spreadsheets &

    2 & Developing & File-based integration (CSV/Excel exports); partial automation & 
    Semi-manual; scheduled exports & Basic standards; inconsistent\\
    3 & Defined & API-based point-to-point integration; automated data flows & 
    Mostly automated; near real-time & Defined processes; mostly consistent\\

    Fully automated; real-time dashboards & Fully standardized; continuous improvement\\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Level 1 --- Initial.} At this level, PMO environments rely entirely on 
manual data consolidation. Project data is collected from multiple disconnected 
tools and compiled into reports manually, typically using spreadsheets. There is 
no systematic data flow between systems, resulting in high risk of errors, 
inconsistencies, and reporting delays. Governance practices are ad hoc and 
unstandardized.

\textbf{Level 2 --- Developing.} At Level 2, some degree of semi-automated 
integration exists through file-based data exchange, such as scheduled CSV or 
Excel exports between systems. While this reduces some manual effort, significant 
human intervention is still required to consolidate and validate data. Reporting 
is semi-automated and governance standards are partially defined but inconsistently 
applied.

\textbf{Level 3 --- Defined.} Level 3 organizations have implemented API-based 
point-to-point integrations between key PMO systems, enabling automated data 
flows between project tracking, financial, and reporting tools. Reporting is 
mostly automated and near real-time. Governance processes are clearly defined 
and consistently followed across most PMO functions.

\textbf{Level 4 --- Optimized.} At the highest level of maturity, PMO environments 
operate within a unified platform ecosystem where all systems are fully integrated 
and data flows in real time. Centralized BI dashboards provide complete portfolio 
visibility, and governance processes are fully standardized with continuous 
improvement mechanisms in place. This level represents the benchmark for 
information system integration excellence in PMO environments.

\subsection{Variables and Relationships}
The model defines the following key variables and relationships that will be 
tested empirically in Chapter~3:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Independent variable:} Integration Maturity Level (1--4), 
    operationalized through survey items measuring integration mechanisms, 
    automation level, and BI centralization.
    \item \textbf{Dependent variables:} Reporting automation degree, perceived 
    data accuracy and timeliness, and governance standardization level.
    \item \textbf{Expected relationships:} Higher integration maturity levels 
    are expected to be positively associated with all three dependent variables, 
    consistent with the hypotheses stated in the Introduction.
\end{itemize}
\section{Research Motivation and Direction}
\label{sec:motivation}

\paragraph{Problem significance.}
The fragmentation of information systems in PMO environments represents a 
significant operational challenge for Latvian enterprises. When project tracking, 
financial, and reporting tools operate in isolation, PMO staff are forced to 
spend substantial time on manual data consolidation, increasing the risk of 
errors and reducing the timeliness of management reporting. This inefficiency 
directly affects the quality of strategic decision-making at the portfolio level. 
The stakeholders most affected include PMO managers, project sponsors, and 
executive leadership who rely on accurate and timely project data to make 
investment and resource allocation decisions.

\paragraph{Identified gap.}
As established in Section~\ref{sec:related}, existing research on IS integration 
maturity does not specifically address PMO environments, and no empirical study 
has examined this phenomenon in the Latvian enterprise context. The gap can be 
stated precisely as follows: \emph{the distribution of IS integration maturity 
levels across PMO environments in Latvian enterprises, and its relationship with 
reporting automation, data quality, and governance standardization, has not been 
empirically measured or documented.} This gap is both testable and measurable 
through survey-based quantitative research.

\paragraph{Direction and scope.}
This thesis addresses the identified gap by conducting an empirical survey-based 
study of IS integration maturity in PMO environments across medium and large 
Latvian enterprises. The study applies the four-level Integration Maturity Model 
proposed in Section~\ref{sec:theory} as the primary measurement framework. The 
scope is deliberately bounded to enterprises with 50 or more employees that 
maintain a formal PMO structure, as smaller organizations are unlikely to have 
sufficiently complex IS landscapes to make integration maturity a meaningful 
concern. The study does not examine individual project outcomes, software 
implementation processes, or the technical architecture of specific integration 
solutions — these are explicitly out of scope. This direction directly corresponds 
to the Aim and Tasks defined in the Introduction.

\paragraph{Success criteria.}
The study will be considered successful if it achieves the following outcomes: 
(1) a valid and reliable survey instrument is developed and administered to at 
least 30 respondents from Latvian enterprises with formal PMO structures; 
(2) the distribution of integration maturity levels across the sample is 
described using descriptive statistics; (3) statistically significant 
relationships between integration maturity and the dependent variables 
(reporting automation, data quality, governance standardization) are tested 
using correlation and regression analysis; and (4) practical recommendations 
for PMO IS integration improvement are formulated based on the empirical findings.

\section{Chapter Summary}
\label{sec:summary}

This chapter established the theoretical and conceptual foundations of the thesis. 
Section~\ref{sec:concepts} defined eight key terms — PMO, PMIS, IS integration, 
integration maturity model, business intelligence, data quality, reporting 
automation, and medium/large enterprise — providing a consistent conceptual 
vocabulary for the remainder of the thesis. Section~\ref{sec:related} surveyed 
five thematic strands of related research, contrasting traditional PMO research, 
API integration studies, BI-governance research, maturity model frameworks, and 
Baltic digital maturity research, and identified a concrete empirical gap. 
Section~\ref{sec:theory} proposed a four-level Integration Maturity Model as 
the primary theoretical framework, defining the variables and relationships 
to be tested empirically. Section~\ref{sec:motivation} articulated the problem 
significance, the measurable research gap, the scope of the study, and the 
success criteria.

\noindent\textbf{Conclusions for Chapter~1.}
The literature review confirms that while substantial research exists on PMO 
governance, IS integration architectures, and digital maturity models, no 
empirical study has specifically examined IS integration maturity in PMO 
environments within Latvia. The proposed four-level Integration Maturity Model 
provides a theoretically grounded and operationalizable framework for addressing 
this gap. These foundations motivate the research methodology presented in 
Chapter~2 and the empirical study conducted in Chapter~3.
