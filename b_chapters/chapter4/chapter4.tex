\chapter{Discussion and Recommendations}
\label{chap:discussion}

This chapter interprets the empirical findings presented in Chapter~3 
in the context of the theoretical framework and existing literature 
reviewed in Chapter~1. It discusses the significance of the results, 
compares them with prior research, formulates practical recommendations 
for improving information system integration maturity in PMO environments 
in Latvian enterprises, and reflects on unexpected findings and 
theoretical implications.

\section{Discussion of Findings}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Integration Maturity Distribution in Latvian PMOs}
The survey results revealed that 70\% of the sampled PMO environments 
operate at intermediate integration maturity levels — either Level 2 
(Developing) or Level 3 (Defined). This finding is consistent with 
Hypothesis 1 and aligns with broader research on digital maturity 
in Baltic enterprises, which suggests that Latvian organizations 
generally lag behind Nordic counterparts in terms of digital 
integration and automation \cite{eurostat2023}.

The dominance of Levels 2 and 3 suggests that most Latvian enterprises 
have moved beyond completely manual data consolidation but have not yet 
achieved full platform-level integration. This is a critical observation 
because it indicates that the majority of organizations are in a 
transitional phase — they have recognized the need for integration 
but have not yet made the full investment required to reach Level 4. 
This finding is also consistent with the expert interview, in which 
Expert 1 noted that even organizations with relatively high integration 
maturity still face challenges such as data latency and legacy system 
resistance.

\subsection{Integration Maturity and Reporting Automation}
The statistically significant positive correlation between integration 
maturity and reporting automation (r=0.405, p=0.010) confirms that 
organizations with more advanced IS integration generate reports more 
automatically. This finding supports Hypothesis 2 and is consistent 
with research by Hohpe and Woolf \cite{hohpe2004}, who demonstrated 
that API-based integration architectures significantly reduce the need 
for manual data handling.

The practical implication of this finding is clear: organizations that 
invest in higher levels of IS integration can expect a meaningful 
reduction in the time and effort spent on manual reporting. Expert 1 
confirmed this, describing a 70/30 split in favour of automated 
reporting in their organization, compared to what is likely a much 
lower automation rate in Level 1 and Level 2 organizations.

\subsection{Integration Maturity and Data Quality}
The positive correlation between integration maturity and data quality 
(r=0.321, p=0.043), while the weakest of the three, remains 
statistically significant. This confirms Hypothesis 3 and supports 
the theoretical argument made in Chapter~1 that manual data 
consolidation introduces errors and delays that undermine data quality 
\cite{dama2017}.

The relatively weaker correlation for data quality compared to 
reporting automation and governance standardization may reflect the 
fact that data quality is influenced by many factors beyond IS 
integration alone — including user behaviour, data entry discipline, 
and organizational culture. Expert 1 highlighted this point, noting 
that even with high integration maturity, data quality depends heavily 
on how consistently project managers update their data. This suggests 
that technical integration alone is not sufficient — it must be 
accompanied by organizational practices that support data hygiene.

\subsection{Integration Maturity and Governance Standardization}
The strong positive correlation between integration maturity and 
governance standardization (r=0.403, p=0.010) confirms Hypothesis 4 
and provides empirical support for the theoretical link between 
centralized BI systems and governance quality established in 
Chapter~1 \cite{negash2004}. Organizations with higher integration 
maturity demonstrated significantly stronger governance standardization 
practices.

This finding is strongly supported by the expert interview. Expert 1 
described how the introduction of a centralized Power BI Command 
Center transformed governance in their organization by enforcing 
universal KPI definitions, eliminating subjective reporting, and 
making project performance transparently visible to all executives 
simultaneously. This qualitative evidence provides important context 
for understanding the mechanism through which higher integration 
maturity leads to better governance standardization.

\section{Comparison with Existing Literature}
\label{sec:literature-comparison}

The findings of this study are largely consistent with existing 
research on IS integration and PMO maturity. The dominance of 
intermediate maturity levels in the sample aligns with research 
by Hobbs and Aubry \cite{hobbs2007}, who found that most PMOs 
struggle to advance beyond basic standardization practices. The 
positive relationships between integration maturity and the three 
dependent variables are consistent with the theoretical predictions 
of the Integration Maturity Model proposed in Chapter~1.

However, this study makes a unique contribution by providing 
empirical evidence specifically from the Latvian enterprise context. 
As noted in Chapter~1, no prior study had specifically examined IS 
integration maturity in PMO environments within Latvia. The findings 
suggest that Latvian enterprises follow a similar pattern to those 
documented in broader European research, but with a tendency toward 
lower overall maturity levels, which is consistent with the digital 
maturity gap between Baltic and Nordic countries identified by 
Eurostat \cite{eurostat2023}.

\section{Unexpected Findings and Theoretical Implications}
\label{sec:implications}

\subsection{Unexpected Findings}
One noteworthy finding was that data quality showed the weakest 
correlation with integration maturity (r=0.321) compared to 
reporting automation (r=0.405) and governance standardization 
(r=0.403). This was somewhat unexpected, as the theoretical 
framework suggested that automated integration would have a 
strong direct impact on data accuracy by eliminating manual 
entry errors. However, as Expert 1 observed, data quality is 
also strongly influenced by human behaviour and organizational 
discipline — factors that are independent of technical integration 
level. This suggests that the relationship between IS integration 
and data quality is more complex than initially theorized and 
warrants further investigation in future research.

Another interesting observation is that 15\% of organizations 
operated at Level 4 — the highest maturity level. This is higher 
than might be expected given the generally lower digital maturity 
of Baltic enterprises compared to Nordic counterparts. This may 
reflect a degree of self-selection bias in the sample, as 
organizations with more advanced PMO structures may have been 
more willing to participate in a PMO-focused survey.

\subsection{Theoretical Implications}
This study makes several contributions to the theoretical 
understanding of IS integration maturity in PMO environments. 
First, it provides empirical validation of the four-level 
Integration Maturity Model proposed in Chapter~1, demonstrating 
that the model's levels correspond to meaningful differences in 
reporting automation, data quality, and governance standardization. 
Second, it extends the existing literature on digital maturity 
models by applying the maturity model concept specifically to 
PMO IS integration — a context that had not previously been 
examined empirically. Third, it provides the first empirical 
evidence of IS integration maturity patterns in Latvian enterprises, 
contributing to the growing body of research on digital 
transformation in Baltic and Eastern European contexts.

\section{Practical Recommendations}
\label{sec:recommendations}

Based on the empirical findings and the expert interview insights, 
the following practical recommendations are proposed for medium and 
large Latvian enterprises seeking to improve their PMO IS integration 
maturity.

\subsection{Recommendation 1 --- Prioritize API-Based Integration}
Organizations currently at Level 1 or Level 2 should prioritize 
the implementation of API-based connections between their key PMO 
systems — particularly between project tracking tools, financial 
systems, and reporting platforms. Even basic API integration can 
significantly reduce manual data consolidation and improve reporting 
automation, as evidenced by the strong correlation between maturity 
level and reporting automation found in this study.

\subsection{Recommendation 2 --- Invest in Centralized BI Dashboards}
The strongest practical step organizations can take to improve 
governance standardization is to implement a centralized BI or 
dashboard solution. Tools such as Power BI, Tableau, or Smartsheet 
can aggregate data from multiple systems into a single view, 
enforcing consistent KPI definitions and making project performance 
transparent across the organization. As Expert 1 noted, this single 
change can be transformative for governance quality.

\subsection{Recommendation 3 --- Consider Low-Code Integration Platforms}
For medium-sized Latvian enterprises that cannot afford large-scale 
enterprise integration solutions, low-code and modular platforms 
such as Monday.com, ClickUp, or Zapier offer a cost-effective path 
toward higher integration maturity. These tools allow organizations 
to connect their existing systems without significant technical 
expertise or large financial investment.

\subsection{Recommendation 4 --- Address Local System Interoperability}
Many Latvian enterprises use local accounting and ERP software that 
is not natively compatible with global PM tools. Organizations should 
prioritize building or procuring standardized connectors between 
these local systems and their PMO tools to eliminate the manual 
data exports that currently bridge this gap.

\subsection{Recommendation 5 --- Invest in Data Literacy}
Technical integration alone is not sufficient to improve data quality. 
Organizations must also invest in training project managers and PMO 
staff to understand the importance of data hygiene and to use 
integrated systems effectively. As Expert 1 observed, the 
introduction of a Data Health Score system helped maintain data 
accuracy even in a highly integrated environment.

\section{Limitations of the Study}
\label{sec:study-limitations}

While the findings of this study are meaningful and statistically 
significant, several limitations should be considered when 
interpreting the results. The sample consisted of 40 respondents, 
which is sufficient for the planned analyses but limits the 
statistical power of more advanced models. The reliance on 
self-reported data means that respondents may have overestimated 
their organization's integration maturity. The cross-sectional 
design captures a snapshot in time and cannot establish causal 
relationships. Finally, the sample is dominated by IT sector 
organizations, which may limit the generalizability of findings 
to other industries. These limitations are discussed in more 
detail in Chapter~2.

\section{Chapter Summary}
\label{sec:chap4summary}

This chapter discussed the empirical findings of the study in the 
context of the theoretical framework and existing literature. All 
four hypotheses were confirmed by the data, with statistically 
significant positive correlations found between IS integration 
maturity and reporting automation, data quality, and governance 
standardization. The findings are consistent with broader research 
on PMO maturity and digital transformation in Baltic enterprises. 
An unexpected finding was the relatively weak correlation between 
integration maturity and data quality, which suggests that human 
behaviour and organizational culture play an important role alongside 
technical integration. Five practical recommendations were formulated 
to help Latvian enterprises improve their PMO IS integration maturity. 
These findings and recommendations form the basis for the conclusions 
presented in the following chapter.
